What is a deductible in insurance? +

A deductible is the amount you are responsible for paying out-of-pocket before your insurance coverage kicks in. For example, if you have a $500 deductible and your total claim is $2,000, you pay the first $500 while your insurer covers the remaining $1,500. Deductibles vary by policy and type of insurance‚Äîhigher deductibles typically mean lower premiums, but you assume more financial risk in the event of a claim. It‚Äôs essential to choose a deductible amount that aligns with your financial capacity and risk tolerance. Some policies, like health insurance, may have both individual and family deductibles.

  • Health Insurance: Covers medical expenses, including doctor visits, hospital stays, and prescription drugs.
  • Auto Insurance: Protects against financial loss in case of car accidents, theft, or damage to your vehicle.
  • Home Insurance: Provides coverage for damage to your home and belongings due to theft, fire, or natural disasters.
  • Life Insurance: Offers financial support to your beneficiaries in case of your death, helping to cover funeral costs and future living expenses.
  • Liability Insurance: Covers legal costs and damages if you‚Äôre held responsible for injury or property damage to others.
